## What Mike decided

**Tropo-OS license changes from AGPL-3 to Apache 2.0.** Confirmed 2026-05-16 after reading the [OSS License Research note (b7e9c41d)](b7e9c41d.md). This is a deliberate reversal of the previously-locked AGPL-3 choice (current LICENSE at `argo-os/.tropo/templates/root-docs/LICENSE`, ship-artifact entry [4a7e1c9b](4a7e1c9b.md), originally locked 2026-05-08 era).

## Why Mike reversed it

The AGPL-3 choice was thoughtful and aligned with the verification-is-the-moat thesis + the frontier-model-training research ([e07bc98a](e07bc98a.md)). But after walking through the research with Talos T4:

1. **The verification moat lives in the substrate design (capsules, audit trails, governance discipline), not in the license.** Anyone training on Apache-licensed Tropo gets the code, but they don't get the substrate-pattern discipline — that's the actual moat.
2. **Tropo isn't fighting cloud vendors over a managed-service revenue stream.** The AGPL defensive posture is solving a problem Tropo doesn't have. The motion is "thousands of Studios," which optimizes for adoption velocity, not commercial-license enforcement.
3. **AGPL is blanket-banned at most F500 companies** for unbounded-obligation reasons. The "thousands of Studios eventually including enterprise" long-term vision is in direct tension with AGPL's enterprise-adoption friction.
4. **Apache 2.0 carries the patent grant** that protects against submarine patent claims in the active-AI-patent-filing landscape. MIT was also considered but lacks the patent grant — Apache 2.0 is asymmetrically better at zero practical cost.
5. **Apache 2.0 is the de facto standard for serious AI/agent frameworks** released by commercial backers in 2023-2026: Mistral, OpenAI's SDK, Letta, Mem0, Continue, Cline, Cody, Haystack, Vercel AI SDK. Tropo joins that pattern cleanly.

The frontier-model-training research finding that copyleft is "the contested zone" was acknowledged but downweighted — the lab fair-use legal theory is consistent regardless of license, and the practical adoption cost of AGPL outweighs the theoretical defensive benefit for a markdown-substrate framework.
