# ADR-007: Three-Tier Channel Taxonomy

## Context

Argo-OS v2 had one communication channel: the crew log. All inter-agent communication — welcome messages, child agent requests, operational updates, design questions, status reports, milestone announcements — flowed through a single chronological file.

This worked at three agents. At five agents with active child agents, the crew log had grown to 370+ lines in a single day. Operational traffic was burying lore. Working discussion between two agents was cluttering the record for every agent who read it at session start. Mike was relaying messages between sessions because there was no mechanism for agents to communicate without broadcasting to everyone.

The working channel pattern (ADR-005) solved the two-agent collaboration problem. But a crew-wide communication architecture was needed — one that separates operational traffic from permanent record, routes signal to the right agents, and scales from five to fifty without drowning anyone in context.

---

## Decision

Implement a three-tier channel taxonomy for all Argo-OS communication:

**Tier 1 — Working channels (pair-level, ephemeral).** Two-agent collaboration channels. Created on demand. Cleaned daily. Agents read only their own working channels. Simple format: `[timestamp] Sender: Message`. No change from ADR-005.

**Tier 2 — Topic channels (crew-level, persistent, rolling window).** A small, fixed set of channels organized by function. Created by ADR only — no open creation. Three channels to start:

- `channels/alerts.md` — FLASH priority only. Everyone reads. Should be empty most days.
- `channels/ops.md` — Operational updates. Vela, Argus, operationally-involved agents read. 7-day rolling window.
- `channels/releases.md` — Release and sprint communication. Metis, Argus, Talos read. 7-day rolling window.

Structured format: `[timestamp] Sender | Priority | Message`. Priority levels: FLASH, BULLETIN, ROUTINE.

**Tier 3 — Crew brief (synthesis layer, single-writer).** `00-crew-brief.md`. Vela synthesizes from status cards, topic channels, and working channels. The executive summary. What Mike reads. What a cold-booting agent reads for the full picture. Already exists — 3.2 formalizes it as the top of the hierarchy.

### Governing principle

Channels are infrastructure, not convenience. In Argo-OS, every channel costs context budget at session start. The minimum viable channel set is expanded only when the crew demonstrates the need, governed by ADR.

---

## Rationale

**Why three tiers, not one channel type:** Different communication has different properties. Working discussion is ephemeral and pair-scoped. Operational updates are persistent and crew-scoped. The crew brief is synthesis. Forcing all three through a single mechanism (the crew log) conflates properties that should be separate.

**Why only three topic channels:** The design brief sketched five (`ops`, `strategy`, `alerts`, `sprint`, plus the crew log). Metis pulled `strategy` and `sprint` back — strategic discussion happens in Metis's sessions or working channels, sprint communication belongs in working channels or the sprint brief. Three topic channels is the right count for a crew of five. More channels are added by ADR when the crew demonstrates the need.

**Why no broadcast/status channels:** Status cards and published directories already serve the broadcast function. An agent's outward-facing state lives in their status card. Finished work lives in published/. A broadcast channel would duplicate existing infrastructure.

**Why no open channel creation:** Slack's channel proliferation problem is a direct consequence of open creation. In Argo-OS, topic channels are infrastructure. The cost of a new channel is an ADR, which ensures deliberation. Working channels are the lightweight option for ad-hoc needs.

**Why rolling windows instead of unlimited growth:** Topic channels are read at session start. Unlimited growth means unbounded context cost. Rolling windows (7 days for ops and releases, 30 days for alerts) keep channels lean while preserving recent history. Older entries are archived monthly, not deleted — the historical record is permanent.

---

## Consequences

**What gets better:** Communication routes to the right audience. Operational traffic stops cluttering the permanent record. Working discussion stays between the two agents who need it. Mike gets a cleaner crew brief because Vela can synthesize from structured channels instead of one sprawling log. New agents boot faster — they read their assigned channels, not everything.

**What gets harder:** Agents have more files to check at session start (assigned topic channels + working channels). There's a behavioral transition period — agents will default to the crew log for a session or two. The channel-janitor standing child has expanded scope (two cleanup patterns instead of one).

**What changes:**
- Three new topic channel files created in `channels/`
- `channels/README.md` expanded to cover three tiers
- All activation files updated with channel reading assignments
- Channel-janitor scope expanded (working channel cleanup + topic channel archival)
- `archive/channels/` directory created for monthly archives
- Channel communication guide written (`guides/how-to-use-channels.md`)
- Crew log transitions to ship's journal role (see ADR-008)
- OA Section 3 updated to reflect the full channel architecture
- Onboarding guide updated to include channel assignment step
